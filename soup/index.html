What am I looking at

Every pixel is a 128-byte program running a tiny virtual machine. There is no fitness function and no goal — programs just execute, and one of the things a program can do is write bytes into a neighbour. Out of the noise, a program occasionally arises that writes a copy of itself into its neighbours. That's a replicator, and it spreads. Nothing designed it and nothing rewards it — the replication is fully emergent.

How this is different

This is our own substrate, not a reimplementation. The emergence you're watching echoes Agüera y Arcas et al.'s Computational Life (2024) — but that's the starting line, not the destination. Two things set ours apart. The environment is the real GPU: an organism's world is the actual cost landscape of the silicon — memory locality, and the real time each operation takes — so life evolves to exploit the hardware it runs on. And replication is the floor, not the goal: we're building toward a soup that evolves learning rules and credit assignment — neural networks that grow and adapt — under a metabolic economy where doing useful computation earns the energy to keep running, so selection can favour computation and cooperation, not only copying. (This browser version is a simplified slice of that larger substrate.)

Color is identity, not a fixed key: a smooth patch of one hue is a family of near-identical replicators (one lineage); a sharp boundary is where two lineages meet. Watching the colors shift is watching lineages mutate, spread, and replace each other.

The readouts: HOE (higher-order entropy) is the complexity score — near 0 for random code, climbing to a few bits once structured replicators take over. diversity is how many distinct genomes survive, kin measures spatial family structure, and faithful / lossy / partial split how cleanly programs actually copy themselves — from near-perfect self-copies (≥90%) through imperfect ones down to partial 25–60% copies — measured by running each program. Turn 2-D topology off to watch a single monoculture sweep the grid instead; the mutation slider adds the variation that keeps things evolving rather than freezing.

A Mythos Scientific substrate — a self-modifying bytecode VM, one program per pixel, no fitness function, live on your GPU via WebGPU. Related work: Agüera y Arcas et al., Computational Life (2024).
